1. Executive Summary

AySA (Agua y Saneamientos Argentinos S.A.) is Argentina's largest water and sewage utility, serving ~14 million people across Buenos Aires City (CABA) and 26 municipalities of the Greater Buenos Aires metropolitan area. Founded in 2006 after the rescission of the Aguas Argentinas (Suez Group) concession, AySA is currently 90% state-owned and undergoing a privatization process (public tender launched May 2026, offers due Aug 27, 2026), subject to a judicial injunction.

5.5 Why Only 15% of Homes Have Meters?

Root cause: Argentina's water billing system was built in 1912 around property characteristics, not consumption. Unlike gas or electricity — which always required meters — water was treated as an unlimited public good, billed by the square meter of the building. This "flat-rate by area" culture persisted for over a century.
BarrierExplanation
1. Historical legacy (1912–2016)From Obras Sanitarias de la Nación (1912) through Aguas Argentinas (1993–2006) to early AySA, billing was always based on property parameters (m², construction type, zone, age). No infrastructure for mass metering existed.
2. Condominium complexityMost buildings in CABA have a single shared water connection for all units. Installing individual meters requires: (a) independent plumbing per unit (costly renovation), (b) consortium assembly approval, (c) legal alignment with horizontal property regime. Law 26,221 Art. 5 allows AySA to bill the consortium as a whole — removing the incentive for individual metering.
3. Perverse economic incentiveDuring years of heavy state subsidies (users paid only 30% of the real cost), a metered user would often pay more than an unmetered one, because actual consumption (325 L/person/day) far exceeds the assumed consumption embedded in the area-based formula. This created a strong disincentive to request a meter.
4. Missed targetsIn 2016, AySA planned to install 1.5 million meters by 2021 (target: 90% coverage). By 2019, only ~121,000 had been installed (15%). COVID, government changes, and budget constraints halted the rollout. The 70%-by-2023 target was also missed.
5. Aging infrastructureMany properties — especially in the conurbano — have precarious or informal water connections that cannot support a standard meter without civil works.
6. Free meter incentive (2017–2027)Since 2017, AySA offers free meter installation to any user who requests it. Installation charge formally waived until 2027. Despite this, demand remains low due to the factors above.

Key paradox: AySA users pay less per person (USD 3.7/month) than Sabesp (USD 5–7.5) or COPASA (USD 5–7), but consume more than double the water. The explanation lies in the lack of metering: 85% of AySA users pay a flat rate based on property size, with zero incentive to conserve. In Brazil, where 85–95% of users are metered, the progressive tariff structure (higher price per m³ for higher consumption) effectively reduces demand. The combination of under-metering + low tariff + high consumption makes AySA financially fragile and operationally stressed — the central challenge any privatization buyer must address.

12.8 COPASA Privatization — Parallel Update

Breaking (Jun/2026): Equatorial emerged as the sole finalist in the COPASA privatization at R$49.03/share (~R$7.95 billion total).
